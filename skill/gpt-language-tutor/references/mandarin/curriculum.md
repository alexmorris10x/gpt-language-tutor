# Beginner Mandarin curriculum

Use Modern Standard Mandarin, simplified characters, pinyin, and English support. The sequence targets practical speaking and listening rather than test preparation.

## Course outcome

Complete common beginner interactions, ask for clarification, and reuse familiar patterns in changed situations without relying on a complete script.

## Lesson path

| Lesson | Scenario | Communicative outcome |
|---:|---|---|
| 01 | Meeting someone | Greet someone, exchange names, and close politely |
| 02 | A day at the park | Say what activities you want and like to do |
| 03 | Being in a restaurant | Order a simple meal and ask for the bill |
| 04 | Navigating a city | Ask where a place is and follow a simple direction |
| 05 | At a coffee shop | Choose a drink, temperature, size, and dine-in or takeaway |
| 06 | Shopping at a market | Ask prices, choose quantities, and respond politely |
| 07 | Taking a taxi | State a destination and guide the final stop |
| 08 | Checking into a hotel | Find a reservation and ask basic stay questions |
| 09 | A normal day | Describe a short daily routine in sequence |
| 10 | Making plans | Suggest an activity, arrange a time, and accept or decline |
| 11 | Weather and clothes | Describe conditions and say what clothing is needed |
| 12 | At a pharmacy | Describe a simple symptom and ask how medicine is taken |

## Progression rules

- Start at lesson 01 unless observed evidence supports a later entry point.
- Use the lesson order as a default, not a lock. Learner needs may reorder scenarios.
- Carry two or three prior targets into each new scenario.
- After lesson 04, use a lighter review and transfer session when the productive core remains mostly `Prompted`.
- After lesson 08, require at least one repair phrase from an earlier lesson in each changed retry.
- After lesson 12, synthesize evidence before proposing the next phase.

## Pronunciation thread

Do not turn every lesson into a tone lecture. Select at most two priorities based on observed intelligibility. Across the course, sample:

- four tones and neutral tone;
- third-tone combinations and common tone changes in connected speech;
- initials such as `j/q/x`, `zh/ch/sh`, and `z/c/s`;
- finals such as `-n/-ng`, `ü`, and `er` where they occur naturally;
- phrase rhythm and keeping neutral syllables light.

Text-only evidence cannot establish pronunciation quality.
